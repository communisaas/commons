# Org Acquisition Surface

> The landing page earns attention through specificity, not manipulation.

**Status:** Design philosophy — not yet implemented
**Created:** 2026-04-12
**Depends on:** design-system.md, voice.md, PERCEPTUAL-BRIDGE.md, VERIFICATION-LEGIBILITY.md

---

## The Problem

Every other design document assumes an authenticated user inside the product. PERCEPTUAL-BRIDGE.md addresses the org dashboard. ORG-REDESIGN-THESIS.md addresses information hierarchy after login. voice.md covers in-product language.

None of these address the pre-authentication surface — the page that makes someone who runs a school parent coalition, a water district accountability group, or a trade association decide to click "Create organization."

This is a fundamentally different design problem. The dashboard is a weather station. The landing page is a billboard for the weather station — addressed to someone who has never seen weather data and doesn't know they need it.

---

## Who Lands Here

Campaign directors, executive directors of small nonprofits, policy advocates, coalition coordinators. Not market segments — cognitive profiles.

**Overwhelmed.** Doing 3-5 jobs. Evaluating software is not one of them. The page gets 5 seconds before they decide whether to invest attention.

**Skeptical.** They've been pitched 100 tools. Most were vaporware, got acqui-hired, or raised prices after lock-in. Promises mean nothing. Artifacts mean everything.

**Outcome-fixated.** They don't care about features. They care: did the committee respond differently? Did the school board member read the testimony? Did the water board take the public comment seriously?

**Budget-traumatized.** Every dollar justifies itself to a board. $10K/yr for Quorum haunts them. $15/mo for AN feels extractive when the budget is $0. Free is the only number that doesn't require a conversation.

**Emotionally invested.** They chose this work because they care about water rights, school safety, public health, transit equity. The cause is identity, not a job. Tools that understand this earn trust. Tools that feel transactional don't.

## The Cognitive Sequence

The landing page produces one cognitive event: the double-take. "Wait — is this what a staffer actually receives?"

Everything before the specimen creates the conditions for that moment. The specimen delivers it. Everything after catches the visitor and converts the feeling into action.

### Beat 1: The Gap

What they send now vs. what they could send. The visitor doesn't know their current output is weak because they've never seen the alternative.

Show what a staffer currently receives from advocacy platforms: an email count. "312 people signed this petition." No identity verification. No district proof. No audit trail. Filed in general correspondence.

Don't editorialize. Don't say "this is weak." Show it as a staffer would see it — a line item in a spreadsheet of constituent contacts, indistinguishable from astroturf.

The gap creates the condition for the specimen to land.

### Beat 2: The Specimen

The verification packet in full resolution. Not described. Not illustrated. Shown. A document that looks like something you could print and hand to a legislative aide.

The specimen must feel categorically different from the surrounding page — it's evidence, not copy. Monospace type, label-value pairs, attestation footer, hairline rules. The `--surface-artifact` (white) background on the `--surface-base` (warm cream) ground. A found object placed on the page, not generated by it.

The specimen answers the three staffer intake filters from CMF research (see VERIFICATION-LEGIBILITY.md):
1. "Are these my constituents?" — district-level count, geographic spread
2. "Did they knowingly send this?" — individually composed vs. shared, one submission per person
3. "How many real people?" — government ID verified count, address-matched count

### Beat 3: The Coverage

24 boundary types. This is the single fact that eliminates every competitor for local-government groups.

Not as a number buried in a paragraph. As a visible list or structured display that triggers recognition: "Wait — they cover water districts? School boards? Transit authorities?" The moment a local-government org sees their boundary type listed is the moment they realize this tool was built for them.

Action Network covers ~3 boundary types (federal, state, county). Quorum covers federal + state. Nobody covers the 90,887 local government entities. This isn't a feature comparison — it's a market existence proof.

### Beat 4: The Price as Punchline

Price only lands after value is established. After the specimen, after the gap, after the boundary recognition:

Free. $0. Import your supporters and send your first proof today.

Then the tiers for scale. The free tier isn't generosity — it's the structural decision that makes individuals the supply side (see monetization-policy.md). But to the campaign director, it's: "I can try this right now without asking my board."

$75/mo vs. Quorum's $10K+/yr speaks for itself. Don't explain the savings. Show the numbers. The arithmetic IS the argument.

## Visual Principles for This Surface

### Figure-Ground

The specimen is the figure. Everything else is ground. The specimen should be the densest, most detailed thing on the page. Everything else should be sparse, quiet, deferential. White space around the specimen is a frame that says "look at this."

### Rhythm as Pacing

Each beat is one thought. Silence between beats gives the visitor time to feel before the next thought arrives. Generous vertical gaps separate cognitive beats. The page should have more empty space than content. Confidence to leave space empty is a signal.

### Contrast as Hierarchy

The specimen must feel categorically different from surrounding copy. The typographic register (monospace vs. sans-serif), the density, the internal structure (label-value rows, dividers, attestation footer) — all should make the specimen feel like a document placed on the page, not a section of it.

### Progressive Disclosure as Respect

One thing above the fold. Curiosity drives depth. The specimen. Then one sentence about how it's produced. Then the price. That's the above-fold contract.

Feature enumeration (A/B testing, segmentation, patch-through calling, automation, legislative monitoring, coalition networks) does not belong in the opening pitch. It can live behind a threshold — a click, a scroll into secondary territory, a `/features` route.

### Color as Semantic Signal Only

No gradient hero. No colored section backgrounds. The warm cream ground is the surface. Color appears only where it means something: emerald for verified counts in the specimen, teal for route/action links.

## Voice on This Surface

Not "marketing" but "specificity that creates desire."

The acquisition register (defined in voice.md) applies here. The key distinction from product surfaces:

- Product surfaces: confident statements about the user's own data. "248 verified." "CA-12."
- Acquisition surface: confident statements about what the product produces. "This is what a staffer receives." Then the specimen. No adjectives.

### What we say

- "What a staffer receives from your current platform:" → the weak version
- "What a staffer receives from Commons:" → the specimen
- "24 boundary types." Then the list.
- "Free." Then the tiers.

### What we don't say

- "Revolutionary" / "industry-leading" / "the future of advocacy"
- "Trusted by X organizations" (if true later, show names, not counts)
- "Your voice matters" / "Make a difference" / "Join the movement"
- "Unlike other platforms..." (the specimen IS the comparison — don't editorialize it)

## What This Surface Is NOT

- **Feature matrix.** Features are table stakes. Verification packets are not. Show the thing no one else can produce. The features are secondary.
- **Pricing comparison table.** Price is a punchline, not a spreadsheet. Show the tiers after value is established.
- **Testimonial carousel.** Pre-launch: no testimonials exist. Post-launch: show the artifact a real org produced, not a quote about how they feel about Commons.
- **Animated hero with abstract shapes.** The specimen IS the hero. It doesn't need illustration because it IS the thing.
- **"Trusted by X organizations" bar.** Social proof by count is the same vanity metric we're arguing against. If we show social proof, show the orgs by name and what they produced.
- **Dense prose enumerating capabilities.** Two paragraphs with bolded nouns is how every SaaS page lists features. The visitor classifies the page before reading a word. "SaaS landing page. Scroll. Maybe later." Close tab.

## Segment-Specific Recognition

The four beachhead segments (from product-roadmap.md) have different recognition triggers:

| Segment | Recognition Moment |
|---------|-------------------|
| Domain-obsessed local groups | See their boundary type in the coverage list (water districts, school boards, transit authorities) |
| Science/health advocacy | See "government ID verified" in the specimen — credibility IS their product |
| Conservative/nonpartisan groups | See "Free" + no ideological gatekeeping — the pricing section implicitly includes them |
| Single-issue orgs | See the specimen framing their issue domain (the example should rotate or be parameterizable) |

The current page uses one specimen example (water district). Consider whether the specimen should be parameterizable by URL parameter (e.g., `/org?domain=education` renders a school board specimen) or whether one well-chosen example serves all segments.
